# Pip’s Picks: Body-part board books (4–6 months)

## Educational shift
At 4–6m hands and feet become visible toys; books help attach words to the discovery.

## Top 10 Ember Picks
1. **Where Is Baby's Belly Button?** — Best for tummy flaps
   - Karen Katz Where Is Babys Belly Button board book that names tummy, hands and feet with simple flaps to lift together on the changing mat.
   - Why Pip: Body words land best when you touch the real tummy while the flap lifts. Keep it a giggle game on the changing mat, not a quiz. One or two flaps is enough; stop while smiles are still easy and put the book aside.
2. **Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes** — Best for song body map
   - A Childs Play Head Shoulders Knees and Toes board book pairing the familiar song with body pictures for pointing and gentle touch together.
   - Why Pip: Song plus touch makes knees and toes stick without sounding like a lesson. Sing once, touch once, then stop while they still smile. Keep the book as a prompt on the mat rather than a full workout through every verse.
3. **Ten Little Fingers and Ten Little Toes** — Best for finger and toe rhyme
   - Mem Fox Ten Little Fingers and Ten Little Toes board book celebrating babies hands and feet with warm rhyme and clear pictures on sturdy boards.
   - Why Pip: Fingers and toes are the body parts they can finally see and grab. Count aloud while they hold a foot; the book is the refrain. Keep one short count, then stop while the shared look still feels friendly on the sofa.
4. **From Head to Toe** — Best for move your body pages
   - Eric Carle From Head to Toe board book inviting babies to copy simple animal movements from head nods to foot stamps with a parent on the mat.
   - Why Pip: Body awareness grows when movement joins the name. Do one animal action on the mat, then close the book before it becomes a workout. Stay close, keep it playful, and stop while smiles are still easy after that single action.
5. **The Nose, Toes and Tummy Book** — Best for nose toes play
   - Sally Nicholls Nose Toes and Tummy board book that invites families to press, tickle and name body parts with bold friendly art on sturdy pages.
   - Why Pip: Playful prompts beat dry labels. Beep a nose, then kiss a tummy; the page is permission to be silly together during nose toes play on the mat. Keep one prompt per sitting and stop while giggles are still easy rather than racing every page.
6. **Baby Touch: Faces** — Best for face looking
   - A Ladybird Baby Touch Faces board book of large friendly faces, the first body map many babies notice before hands and feet on the page.
   - Why Pip: Faces are still the strongest body cue. Point to eyes and mouth on the page, then on your own face, then stop. Keep turns short on the sofa and put the book away while the shared look still feels friendly.
7. **Baby Touch: Animals** — Best for animal body shapes
   - A Ladybird Baby Touch Animals board book with clear animal shapes that invite pointing at heads, tails and feet beside your babys own body.
   - Why Pip: Animals give a second route into body words when face books feel finished. Name one part per page, then close the covers. Stay close on the sofa and stop while the shared look still feels friendly rather than finishing every animal shape tonight.
8. **That's Not My Puppy...** — Best for textured puppy body
   - Usborne That is Not My Puppy with soft textured patches that invite stroking ears, tummies and paws while you name the body part aloud together.
   - Why Pip: Texture plus naming keeps hands busy while mouths want everything on the puppy body pages. One patch per sitting is enough. Close the book while the shared look still feels friendly and save the rest of the patches for another awake spell.
9. **That's Not My Cat...** — Best for textured cat body
   - Usborne That is Not My Cat with soft textured patches for ears, whiskers and paws, a second texture set for naming body parts on animals.
   - Why Pip: If Puppy is sticky with milk, Cat keeps the same refrain with new textured patches on the body pages. Cap Usborne at two on this card so the shelf does not fill with the same pattern. Stop while the shared look still feels friendly.
10. **Brown Bear, Brown Bear, What Do You See?** — Best for colour animal chorus
   - Brown Bear colour animal chorus for pointing at eyes, mouths and feet in bold Carle pictures during a short shared look on the sofa.
   - Why Pip: Not a pure body book, but pointing at animal faces and feet still trains the look and name habit with a colour animal chorus. Use when dedicated body titles are all sticky. Keep one animal, then stop while the shared look still feels friendly.
