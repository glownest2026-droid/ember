**Cluster:** Questions, stories and bigger chats · **Buyer mode:** Good gift

## Educational shift

At nearly three, your child stops just naming things in a book and starts leading the page — picking, explaining, predicting what happens next, and arguing their case. Versus **31-33m** (following a simple story arc) and **28-30m** (naming and pointing), the shift is talk-back: books that hand the child a question, a choice or a "what happens next" moment, not ones they only listen to.

### 1. You Choose — Best for open choices
The child picks a face, house, outfit and pet on every spread, then explains why. **Verdict:** Still the cleanest fit for pick-and-explain talk — but check the shelf and nursery book box first, as this one turns up almost everywhere.

### 2. Don't Let the Pigeon Drive the Bus! — Best for saying no
A pleading pigeon keeps asking to drive; the child has to say no and hold their ground. **Verdict:** Less choice-led than You Choose, but brilliant for a child who wants to talk back with confidence.

### 3. Oh No, George! — Best for guessing what's next
George the dog wants to be good but keeps giving in; the child predicts what happens next. **Verdict:** A proper prediction loop, page after page — good for a child who likes calling out the answer early.

### 4. Shh! We Have a Plan — Best for predicting plans
Four friends creep through the woods with a hushed plan that keeps going wrong. **Verdict:** Quieter than Pigeon or George — better for a calmer bedtime chat about what went wrong and why.

### 5. Shark in the Park — Best for is-it-a-shark guesses
Timmy spots something through his telescope and asks "is it a shark?" on repeat. **Verdict:** The lightest, most repeatable pick — a good warm-up for a tired evening when the other four feel too talky.

## What we skipped and why

- **The Gruffalo** — likely already owned; passive rhyme, not question-led (bring back out)
- **Dear Zoo** — baby flap board book; naming/revealing only
- **Press Here** — an earlier-band (28-30m) cause-and-effect pick
- **There's a Monster in Your Book** — earlier-band tactile interactive (shake/wiggle), not explanation-led
- **Little Dinosaurs, Big Questions** — KS1+ philosophy anthology, too advanced for nearly-three sofa chats
- **We're Going on a Bear Hunt** — chant-along classic most families own; repetition without questioning
- **The Very Hungry Caterpillar (board)** — baby counting/sequence book, not talk-back

## Borrow / bring back out / hold off guidance

1. **Borrow first** — library holds on You Choose, Pigeon, Oh No George and Shh We Have a Plan cover most needs; buy new only for the one your child asks for twice
2. **Bring back out** — 28-30m naming books (Dear Zoo, Press Here, Very Hungry Caterpillar) still earn their keep for vocabulary, paired with one Top 5 pick for the why-and-explain shift
3. **Daytime vs bedtime** — Pigeon and Shark in the Park are high-energy; save Shh We Have a Plan and A Bit Lost for calmer evening chats
